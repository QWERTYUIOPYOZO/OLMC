Summary Points

Students learn about:

  • The following area of ethical teaching in Islam
    - Environmental ethics

Students learn to:

  • Describe and explain Islamic ethical teachings on Environmental ethics.

 

Islamic Teachings on Environmental Ethics

  • Environmental ethics are in all the major sources of Islam
  • The Qur'an has a number of specific references to ecology
  • Also contains important principles which are applied to the environment
  • Hadiths have a number of other important statements
  • Shari'ah law includes many teachings which explain the application of Qur'anic principles to areas not specifically addressed in the Qur'an
  • Collectively the sources of authority in Islam provide clear teaching and direction in relation to the environment.
  • Environmental concerns are substantially of a modern nature
  • Relate to issues which are peculiar to an industrialised world
  • Such as over population, global warming etc which belong to recent generations Unreasonable to expect the sources to deal specifically with modern issues Principles of Muslim life provide very clear guidance in relation to these issues Some environmental issues are directly addressed in Muslim sources
  • Other teachings are drawn from the application of fundamental principles to modern concerns

Principles of Environmental Ethics

  • The first principle is the notion of trusteeship "khalifa"
  • Allah has placed creation in the trust of human beings.
  • " The world is green and beautiful, and Allah has appointed you his guardian over it" (Hadith) This guardianship
  • Kahlifa involves the appropriate use of the world's resources for the needs of humankind but in a way which nurtures and sustains the environment
  • The environment is seen not as the property of human beings but as their responsibility to care for and nurture
  • Second principle is "tawhid"
  • Tawhid refers to the unity and absolute oneness of Allah
  • By extension it includes the unity and oneness of that which is created by Allah The unity of Allah is clearly reflected in the integrity of the created world
  • The principle of "tawhid" demands that the integrity of the created world is maintained and respected
  • Third principle is that of accountability "akrah"
  • Muslims believe they will be judged by Allah for the way they have undertaken their responsibilities
  • This includes a judgement concerning how they have acted in relation to the environment
  • Muslims believe that they will be questioned by Allah on the way they carried our their responsibilities towards all aspects of creation

The Qur'an and the Environment

  • "It is He that has made you custodians, inheritors of the earth" (surah 6:165)
  • This surah enunciates the principle of "khalifa"
  • Clearly states the relationship between humankind and the rest of creation
  • Highlights that this relationship of responsibility has been ordained by Allah
  • Surah 45:12-13 speaks of all that is the heavens and the earth being subjected to humankind
  • Human beings have the use of the resources of the world
  • They do not have a right to exploit or destroy these resources
  • Their use of the world's resources should be in keeping with the nurturing and sustaining responsibilities of their guardianship
  • Qur'an conveys the important elements of the diversity and balance that exists in nature
  • "And the earth-we have spread it out wide, and placed on it mountains firm, and caused life of every kind to grow on it in a balanced manner, and provided means of livelihood for you as well as for all living beings whose sustenance does not depend on you. " (surah15:19)
  • Human beings have responsibility to use the world's resources in a way which does not diminish the diversity of creation or threaten the balance and harmony that exists
  • Degree of control that human beings can exercise over creation is tempered by the Qur'an recognising that human beings are only one aspect of the wonder of creation
  • "Greater indeed than the creation of man is the creation of the heavens and the earth." (surah 40:57)
  • Text speaks against the possibility of reckless or complete domination of the natural world for the benefit of human beings
  • Anthropocentric view of creation is incompatible with the teaching of the Qur'an
  • The environment is depicted in the Qur'an as praising and exalting Allah
  • "Have you not seen that unto Allah pays adoration whosoever is in the heavens and whosoever is in the earth, and the sun, and the moon, and the stars, and the hills, and the trees, and the beasts, and many of mankind ..." (surah 22:18)
  • "The seven heavens and the earth and all therein praise him and there is not a thing but hymns his praise. See! He is ever clement, forgiving." (surah 17:44)
  • Environment is created by Allah and reflects Allah's greatness
  • Sacredness in the environment which stems from its relationship with Allah
  • To fail to honour this sacredness is to deny the manner of creation and the glory of Allah as creator
  • Many of the other texts in the Qur'an elaborate the theme of Allah as the creator and convey aspects of the principle of human beings as the guardians of creation Some of these texts focus on specific elements such as the rain (surah 21:30) and vegetation (surah 56:63)
  • There are over 700 occasions where the Qur'an includes some specific reflection on nature and the environment

Environmental Hadiths

  • Hadith shows that Muhammad held a deep concern for the natural environment Encouraged Muslims to treat it with respect and to nurture it with care
  • "the earth is green and beautiful and Allah has appointed you his stewards over it" (Hadith)
  • Reiterates the teaching of the Qur'an that human beings have been given the responsibility of guardianship over the natural environment.
  • "the whole earth has been created a place of worship, pure and clean" (Hadith
  • Affirms the sacredness of creation and warns against its defilement
  • By acknowledging that the earth is a place of worship, the Prophet Muhammad is recognising it as a sacred entity
  • Harming the environment is akin to defiling a place of worship
  • Serious affront to Allah and to all Muslims
  • The planting of a tree or the sowing of a field which provides for the needs of people and of animals is an act of love
  • Expectation that Muslims work with the environment in a caring and nurturing way
  • The environment is intended to provide for the needs of human beings
  • Human beings are meant to work with the environment to ensure its sustainability "If a Muslim plants a tree or sow and field and humans and beasts and birds eat from it, all of it is love on his part" (Hadith).

The Environment and Islamic Law

  • Function of "Shari'ah" is to apply the principles of the Qur'an to specific circumstances and situations which are not specifically addressed in the Qur'an Many environmental concerns are modern issues of a post industrialised world Not envisaged at the time of the revelation of the Qur'an
  • Application of Shari'ah law is important in Muslim teaching on the environment
  • Prevents hunting other than to provide for the needs of the community
  • Laws providing for the most humane way of killing animals
  • Teachings about the welfare of animals which relate to the keeping of animals in confinement
  • Animals should not be tied up or kept in unsuitably small spaces
  • They must also be kept in clean and sanitary surroundings.
  • Islamic teaching opposes experimentation on animals other than those that seek to bring genuinely needed benefits to humanity
  • Only then after careful consideration of all alternatives
  • Testing of cosmetic products on animals is not condoned
  • Includes laws governing the growth and size of cities
  • Laws about appropriate uses for land
  • Prohibitions against overstocking grazing land
  • Protection of forests and wildlife
  • Many other issues
  • Forest land, pasture land and wildlife cannot be privately owned
  • Are to be owned by the state and managed for the good of the whole community
  • Shari'ah law was developed centuries ago and as such does not fully address the concerns of the modern environmental crisis
  • Some scholars believe that Islam has been slow to respond to contemporary environmental issues because its laws are yet to be brought to bear on such issues as global warming, greenhouse gas emissions, acid rain etc

Religion and Ecology

  • Extremes in environmental degradation parallel the worst excesses of materialism and secularism
  • They show a contempt for the environment which is in effect a contempt for the creator and a contempt for the natural order
  • Humankind has moved away from the type of community and the type of values which are at the heart of Islam and of other legitimate religious faiths
  • Ecological crisis is seen as both a sign and a symptom of an attitude where the individual seeks to selfishly satisfy their own wants with little or no regard for others
  • Ultimately self destructive
  • Results in the exploitation of the world's resources
  • The exploitation of other people
  • The loss of human dignity and true human values.
  • Solution to the modern environmental crisis will be found only in a radical transformation of values
  • Ultimately submission to the will of Allah

Water - A Key Concern

  • Islam arose in the harsh desert environment of the Arabian peninsular
  • Water was a cherished commodity
  • Religious reflections of Islam often reflect on the element of water
  • Muslim paradise images are of a desert oasis with abundant refreshing water
  • Images of refreshing rain, rivers and fountains of flavoured water to drink
  • The waters flow beneath and throughout the gardens of paradise providing coolness as well as lush greenery
  • Water is seen as synonymous with life
  • It is the source of all life and is regarded as a gift of Allah
  • Musims are reminded that water is a gift and to be preserved and used with care
  • The concern for a sustainable water supply resulted in the determination of forbidden (haraam) areas near water supplies
  • Prohibited to use water excessively or wastefully
  • Water is a requirement of Muslim prayer
  • The ritual washing known as "wudu" is required for purification before each of the obligatory five times of daily prayer
  • Muhammad forbade Muslims to wash more than three times during the ritual washing as this would be wasteful.
  • Muslims regard water as one of the things that should be freely available to all Not permitted to withhold water from one who needs it
  • All people have the right to quench their thirst, to water their animals and to irrigate their crops
  • In today's world many people lack the basic necessity of clean drinking water
  • It is difficult for Muslims to reconcile their teaching on the fundamental rights to water when the reality of pollution and poverty means that millions of people are denied such rights
  • Justice intersects with concerns for the environment
  • Not possible to address concerns for the environment without also addressing concerns about issues of justice, the distribution of wealth and the ownership and use of the world's resources
  • Islamic ethical teaching recognises that matters such as justice and the environment are integrally connected
  • Also recognises that genuine progress will not be achieved without the element of conversion of individuals and societies
  • Not possible to develop a community based on justice and concern for the environment when individuals are selfishly pursuing their own interests to the detriment of others and ultimately the whole of creation
