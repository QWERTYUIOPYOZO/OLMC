Content Description

Islamic Teachings on Environmental Ethics

Major Sources

Teaching on the care of the environment is found within all the major sources of Islam. Principally the Qur'an has a number of specific references to ecology and also contains some important principles which are able to be applied to the environment. The tradition of the Prophet Muhammad (Hadith) has a number of other important statements. Finally, Shari'ah law includes many teachings which explain the application of Qur'anic principles to areas not specifically addressed in the Qur'an.

Thus, collectively, the sources of authority in Islam provide clear teaching and direction to Muslims in their relationship with the environment.

Modern Issues

Environmental concerns are substantially of a modern nature and in many cases relate to issues which are peculiar to an industrialised world. Issues such as over population, global warming etc belong to recent generations. It is unreasonable, therefore, to expect the sources of an ancient religion to deal specifically with these modern issues. However, Islamic scholars have found that the principles of Muslim life do provide very clear guidance in relation to these issues. Therefore, while some environmental issues are addressed in specific terms through Muslim sources, other teachings are drawn from the application of fundamental principles to modern concerns.

Principles of Environmental Ethics

Trusteeship

The first principle which guides Islamic teaching on ecology is the notion of trusteeship. The term "khalifa" means trustee and guardian and it refers to the understanding that in creating the world Allah has placed creation in the trust of human beings.

The tradition of the Prophet Muhammad states "The world is green and beautiful, and Allah has appointed you his guardian over it" (Hadith). This guardianship "kahlifa" involves the appropriate use of the world's resources for the needs of humankind but in a way which nurtures and sustains the environment. The environment is seen not as the property of human beings but as their responsibility to care for and nurture.

Unity and integrity of creation

Another principle is that of "tawhid". Tawhid refers principally to the unity and absolute oneness of Allah yet by extension it includes the unity and oneness of that which is created by Allah. The unity of Allah is clearly reflected in the integrity of the created world and the principle of "tawhid" demands that the integrity of the created world is maintained and respected.

Akrah

A third principle is that of accountability "akrah". Muslims understand that they will be judged by Allah for the way they have undertaken their responsibilities. This will include a judgement concerning how they have acted in relation to the environment. Muslims believe that they will be questioned by Allah on the way they carried our their responsibilities towards all aspects of creation.

The Qur'an and the Environment

Custodians

"It is He that has made you custodians, inheritors of the earth" (surah 6:165). This surah enunciates the principles of human beings as "khalifa" or custodians of the earth. It is one of the important texts which clearly state the relationship between humankind and the rest of creation. It also highlights that this relationship of responsibility has been ordained by Allah or in other word is the will of Allah.

Subjected to humankind

Another example setting out this relationship is found in surah 45:12-13 which speaks of all that is the heavens and the earth being subjected to humankind. While human beings have the use of the resources of the world, they do not have a right to exploit or destroy these resources. Their use of the world's resources should be in keeping with the nurturing and sustaining responsibilities of their guardianship.

Diversity and balance in nature

The Qur'an clearly conveys the important elements of the diversity and balance that exists in nature: "And the earth-we have spread it out wide, and placed on it mountains firm, and caused life of every kind to grow on it in a balanced manner, and provided means of livelihood for you as well as for all living beings whose sustenance does not depend on you. " (surah15:19). Human beings have a fundamental responsibility to use the world's resources in a way which does not diminish the diversity of creation or threaten the balance and harmony that exists.

Human beings in relation to creation

The degree of control that human beings can exercise over creation is tempered by the Qur'an recognising that human beings are only one aspect of the wonder of creation. "Greater indeed than the creation of man is the creation of the heavens and the earth." (surah 40:57). This text clearly speaks against the possibility of reckless or complete domination of the natural world for the benefit of human beings. In fact it has been used to show that an anthropocentric view of creation is incompatible with the teaching of the Qur'an.

The environment is depicted in the Qur'an as praising and exalting Allah. "Have you not seen that unto Allah pays adoration whosoever is in the heavens and whosoever is in the earth, and the sun, and the moon, and the stars, and the hills, and the trees, and the beasts, and many of mankind ..." (surah 22:18).

Another example from the Qur'an conveying a similar idea is found in surah 17:44 "The seven heavens and the earth and all therein praise him and there is not a thing but hymns his praise. See! He is ever clement, forgiving."

Relationship of creation and creator

Texts such as these alert the Muslim to the relationship between Allah and the environment. It is created by Allah and reflects Allah's greatness. Thus there is a sacredness in the environment which stems from its relationship with Allah. To fail to honour this sacredness is to deny the manner of creation and the glory of Allah as creator.

Many of the other texts in the Qur'an elaborate the theme of Allah as the creator and convey aspects of the principle of human beings as the guardians of creation. Some of these texts focus on specific elements such as the rain (surah 21:30) and vegetation (surah 56:63). Scholars have noted that there are over 700 occasions where the Qur'an includes some specific reflection on nature and the environment.

Environmental Hadiths

Concern for environment

The tradition of the Prophet Muhammad shows clearly that he held a deep concern for the natural environment and encouraged Muslims to treat it with respect and to nurture it with care and concern for the future.

The most well known of the Hadiths relating to the environment states "the earth is green and beautiful and Allah has appointed you his stewards over it". This tradition simply reiterates the teaching of the Qur'an that human beings have been given the responsibility of guardianship over the natural environment.

Sacredness

A further example from the tradition of the Prophet Muhammad says "the whole earth has been created a place of worship, pure and clean". In this Hadith, there is an affirmation of the sacredness of creation and an implicit warning against its defilement. By acknowledging that the earth is a place of worship, the Prophet Muhammad is recognising it as a sacred entity. To act in a way that is harmful to the environment is akin to defiling a place of worship which would be a serious affront to Allah and to all Muslims.

Acts of love

A third example shows the Prophet Muhammad teaching that the planting of a tree or the sowing of a field which provides for the needs of people and of animals is an act of love. This Hadith provides an example of the expectation that Muslims would work with the environment in a caring and nurturing way. The environment is intended to provide for the needs of human beings and human beings are meant to work with the environment to ensure its sustainability. "If a Muslim plants a tree or sow and field and humans and beasts and birds eat from it, all of it is love on his part" (Hadith).

The Environment and Islamic Law

Shari'ah

One of the functions of Islamic law "Shari'ah" is to apply the principles enunciated in the Qur'an to specific circumstances and situations which are not specifically addressed in the Qur'an. Many environmental concerns are modern issues of a post industrialised world and were not envisaged at the time of the revelation of the Qur'an. Accordingly, the application of Shari'ah law is extremely relevant and important in Muslim teaching on the environment.

Hunting

Muslim teaching prevents hunting other than to provide for the needs of the community and there are laws providing for the most humane way of killing animals where it is necessary. There are also teachings about the welfare of animals which relate to the keeping of animals in confinement. Animals should not be tied up or kept in unsuitably small spaces. They must also be kept in clean and sanitary surroundings.

Animal experiments

The use of animals for scientific experiments is a matter of some concern and debate. In general, Islamic teaching would oppose experimentation on animals other than those that seek to bring genuinely needed benefits to humanity and only then after careful consideration of all alternatives. Testing of cosmetic products on animals is not condoned.

Many situations

There are many situations that have been addressed in the development of Shari'ah laws. These include laws governing the growth and size of cities, appropriate uses for land, prohibitions against overstocking grazing land, the protection of forests and wildlife and many other issues.

One of the most interesting elements is the laws that state that forest land, pasture land and wildlife cannot be privately owned. Shari'ah law dictates that these elements are to be owned by the state and managed for the good of the whole community.

Modern issues

Much of the body of Shari'ah law was developed centuries ago and as such do not fully address the concerns of the modern environmental crisis. Some scholars believe that Islam has been slow to respond to contemporary environmental issues because its laws are yet to be brought to bear on such issues as global warming, greenhouse gas emissions, acid rain etc.

Religion and Ecology

Materialism and secularism

For Muslims, the current extremes in environmental degradation parallel the worst excesses of materialism and secularism. They show a contempt for the environment which is in effect a contempt for the creator and a contempt for the natural order.

In seeking self fulfillment, humankind has dramatically moved away from the type of community and the type of values which are at the heart of Islam and of other legitimate religious faiths.

Ecological crisis

The ecological crisis is seen as both a sign and a symptom of an attitude where the individual seeks to selfishly satisfy their own wants with little or no regard for others. Such an attitude is ultimately self destructive as it results in the exploitation of the world's resources, the exploitation of other people and the loss of human dignity and true human values.

Muslims believe that the solution to the modern environmental crisis will be found only in a radical transformation of values and ultimately submission to the will of Allah. It is not sufficient to simply speak of ecological measures which superficially address the symptoms without also addressing the fundamental causes of the crisis.

Water - A Key Concern

Desert environment

Given that Islam arose in the harsh desert environment of the Arabian peninsular, it is not surprising that water was a cherished commodity and that the religious reflections of Islam often reflect on the element of water.

Muslim visions of paradise provide images of a desert oasis with abundant refreshing water. There are images of refreshing rain, rivers and fountains of flavoured water to drink. The waters flow beneath and throughout the gardens of paradise providing coolness as well as lush greenery.

Gift of Allah

Water is seen as synonymous with life, it is the source of all life and is regarded as a gift of Allah. In the context of reminding humankind that they are the guardians of creation they are reminded that water is a gift and to be preserved and used with care.

In the time of the Prophet Muhammad, the concern for a sustainable water supply resulted in the determination of forbidden (haraam) areas near water supplies. It was prohibited to use water excessively or wastefully.

Ritual washing

Water is a requirement of Muslim prayer. The ritual washing known as "wudu" is required for purification before each of the obligatory five times of daily prayer. In this context, the Prophet Muhammad forbade Muslims to wash more than three times during the ritual washing as this would be wasteful.

Muslims regard water as one of the things that should be freely available to all and are not permitted to withhold water from one who needs it. According the Shari'ah law, all people have the right to quench their thirst, to water their animals and to irrigate their crops.

Water crisis

The preciousness of water is all the more keenly felt in today's world where so many people lack the basic necessity of clean drinking water. It is difficult for Muslims to reconcile their teaching on the fundamental rights to water when the reality of pollution and poverty means that millions of people are denied such rights.

In this regard the area of justice intersects with concerns for the environment. It is not possible to address concerns for the environment without also addressing concerns about issues of justice, the distribution of wealth and the ownership and use of the world's resources.

Connections

Islamic ethical teaching recognises that matters such as justice and the environment are integrally connected. It also recognises that genuine progress will not be achieved without the element of conversion of individuals and societies. It is not possible to develop a community based on justice and concern for the environment when individuals are selfishly pursuing their own interests to the detriment of others and ultimately the whole of creation.
