Content Description

The relationship between the religion and its society

Stratified societies

Though social organisation varied, Polynesian societies were always stratified to greater or lesser degrees, usually with a chiefly ruling class known as ali'i or arii or ariki, commoners, and a lower or slave class. Though heredity was the most important element in determining social status there was also provision for a person of ability to rise into acceptance into the aristocracy.

Priestly class

Originally the head of a family mediated with the divine realm, but this function became more specialised. As a result a more particular priestly class developed. These were known as Kahuna or tahu'a or tohunga or tufuga. These priests were regarded as experts in spiritual matters and in possession of higher knowledge.

Social structure ethical standards and taboos

There is much variation in the region as to how much the roles of chief and priest were separate or combined, and to what degree they related to civil power.

The relationship between the religion and its society can be seen in three areas. These are the social structure aligned with beliefs in descendents of ancestor heroes, ethical standards aligned with the legendary actions of these heroes and taboos aligned with elements of mythical stories.

Social structure

The alignment between a stratified social structure and the belief in the ancestry of hero figures is very evident in traditional aspects of Polynesian societies. Various monarchs and nobles in Polynesian societies are believed to be the descendents of the pre historic ancestor figures. The tradition follows that these rulers have a semi divine element due to their ancestry and as such can exercise a prerogative not unlike that of a deity. Thus to a certain extent their decisions and their actions are beyond question or challenge.

Tu'i Tonga

In Tongan society the King is regarded as a descendent of 'Aho'eitu the first Tu'i Tonga and the nobles are regarded as descendents of his four brothers. Thus the monarchy and the nobility are all descendent from the god Tangaloa. This ancestry commands respect from the Tongan people who traditionally believe that this stratified society is divinely ordained and that the monarchy and nobles rule benevolently on behalf of the Tongan people.

Democratic reform

As Tongan society has become more westernised, the traditional beliefs have become less prevalent and in recent decades there has been a strong push for democratic reform in Tonga. Nevertheless, democracy advocates have considerable difficulty in convincing villagers in more remote areas that the social stratification of Tongan society is something that can be challenged and/or changed.

Ethical standards

The second important relationship between traditional religion and the society is found in the ethical requirements for living. While there are no formulations of moral codes, there are strong indications in the actions of heroes such as Maui as to the type of ethical behaviour which can be expected.

Oral tradition

In Tongan society, for example, people will comment "that is not the Tongan way" when referring to an act of dishonesty or harm to others. The basis for this statement is not a set of laws or a code setting down appropriate practices. Rather if asked to explain why something is or is not ethical a Tongan is more likely to respond with a story from the oral tradition of their ancestor heroes which illustrates the point.

Taboos

The third area relates to practices which are forbidden or taboo (tapu). Once again these prohibitions do not arise from a set of laws or a code but rather through the lesson drawn from an aspect of the oral tradition.
