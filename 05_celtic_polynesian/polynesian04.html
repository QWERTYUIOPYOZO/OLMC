Content Description

Principal rituals and their significance for the individual and community

Kava ceremony

The most significant ritual practice among Polynesian societies is the Kava or 'Ava ceremony. The ceremony is a traditional ritual of welcome and acknowledgement of the status of visitors and in particular dignitaries. It involves the ritual drinking of a beverage made from the crushed root of the kava plant. The kava is served in a cup often made from a coconut shell with the beverage drawn from a central carved wooden or stone bowl. The most highly esteemed person present is first presented with the kava to drink and then with due ceremony the kava is then shared by each person in order according to their social status.

Origins of the Kava ceremony

The kava ceremony relates back to a mythical beginning when Momo, the King of Tonga and the tenth Tu'i Tonga took part in a failed fishing expedition to the east of the Island of Tongatapu. The royal party returned to shore hungry and in a state of exhaustion. They landed on the small islet of 'Eueki which was inhabited by a poor family of commoner standing.

Offering to the King

The presence of the King obliged the family to make an offering and they intended to harvest their tarot plant for the offering. However, the found the King resting against it and realised therefore that it was now tapu (taboo) because the King had touched it. Having nothing else to offer they decided to offer their daughter Kava'onau as a sacrifice. They killed her and baked her in an 'umu (earth oven).

'Umu becomes a grave

When the King realised what had taken place he ordered the family not to uncover the 'umu but to use it as Kava'onau's grave. In time kava and sugarcane grew from the grave and this was then used to create the kava drink for the formal kava ceremony.

Ceremony serves many purposes

The kava ceremony is singularly the most significant ritual element of Polynesian societies. It serves a variety of purposes from welcome, reconciliation, anointing, atonement etc. There is virtually no significant occasion which would not include a kava ceremony.

A kava ceremony involves strict protocols, particularly relating to the order in which those present are offered the kava cup. The most highly esteemed is served first and then others follow in order of their social status. On occasions where a host has wanted to give offence to a guest or to indicate ill feeling between parties they do so by not offering the kava in the appropriate order. Not offering the kava in the appropriate order is a guaranteed way to give offence.
