Content Description

Principal beliefs of the religion

Protection of individuals and the group

The primary concern of religion in Polynesian societies was protection of the people, individual and group, from spiritual powers. Strict obedience to laws was considered necessary to keep the divine and physical worlds in harmony. The main functions of priests were to access mind of the gods and communicate their requirements. This role enabled priests in order to foretell events and also to determine which ceremonies were needed to ensure the safe ordering of the society.

Aligned with an aspect of nature

The gods of Polynesian societies were frequently aligned with an aspect of nature or with a natural occurrence. For example, the sky god Tawhirimatea sends cyclones to ravage the forests and destroy the crops of the people. These cyclones are believed to be the children of Tawhirimatea whom he sends to earth as an act of vengeance against his brother Tanemahuta who is an earth god. In this example the people are the innocent casualties of a supernatural feud between the gods.

Ancestral heroes

Polynesian beliefs also incorporate a kind of ancestral being and hero figure who is born through the liaison between a god and a human being. These figures are endowed with supernatural powers and are regarded as ancestors to the modern human being.

Embodied spirits

Ancestral spirits were important in everyday life. These, and nature spirits, could be embodied in a variety of objects and great care was taken not to offend them.

After death a person is believed to dwell in a limbo like underworld. In this underworld there is no notion of reward, punishment or judgement. However, the underworld is believed to be unstratified and thus a place where mortal distinctions between noble and commoner no longer applied.
