Content Description

Agnosticism

Core beliefs of agnosticism

Agnosticism is essentially a sceptical worldview, which awaits more conclusive empirical evidence than that which is currently available, before it choses to commit to the belief in the existence of a transcendent reality. Thus, at the present time, agnostics argue that there is insufficient evidence to support the existence of a transcendent reality, though this could conceivably change in the future. Since agnostics, at this point in time, do not believe that evidence to prove the existence of god is sufficient, they do not entertain any idea of heaven, hell, judgement, or any other idea directly related to the belief in a transcendent being.

Atheism

Atheism is not an active belief system with its own set of doctrines

It is difficult to pinpoint exactly how atheism as a worldview shapes the aspirations and behaviours of individuals. This is because atheism, unlike humanism, is not an active philosophy with a body of organised doctrines. Rather, atheism can be defined in the negative as the disbelief in the notion of god or gods. Having said that however, it should be remembered that humanists are atheists and hence atheism and humanism are not two mutually exclusive philosophies.

Overlapping nature of atheism and humanism

Many of the beliefs and aspirations of humanism can also be applied to atheism, however they are not exclusive to or particular to atheism, rather, they are characteristics of humanism that atheists may also possess.

Humanism

Core beliefs of humanism

Humanism is essentially a philosophy based on the premise that this world, this life, and the morals, ethics and experiences gained from this life are all we have. Human beings, therefore, are able to control their own destiny. Since we can control our destinies and that of other people, we should also try to better the lives of others. Consequently, humanism has strong ethical and moral doctrines regarding social responsibility, ecological sustainability, the belief that all human beings are born free and equal. These are some of the core beliefs held by humanists.

Amsterdam Declaration

The Amsterdam Declaration of 2002 is the defining creed of World Humanism. According to the Amsterdam Declaration the fundamental tenets of humanism which determine the aspirations and behaviours of individual humanists are as follows:

  1. Humanism is ethical. It affirms the worth, dignity and autonomy of the individual and the right of every human being to the greatest possible freedom compatible with the rights of others. Humanists have a duty of care to all of humanity including future generations. Humanists believe that morality is an intrinsic part of human nature based on understanding and a concern for others, needing no external sanction.

  2. Humanism is rational. It seeks to use science creatively, not destructively. Humanists believe that the solutions to the world's problems lie in human thought and action rather than divine intervention. Humanism advocates the application of the methods of science and free inquiry to the problems of human welfare. But Humanists also believe that the application of science and technology must be tempered by human values. Science gives us the means but human values must propose the ends.

  3. Humanism supports democracy and human rights. Humanism aims at the fullest possible development of every human being. It holds that democracy and human development are matters of right. The principles of democracy and human rights can be applied to many human relationships and are not restricted to methods of government.

  4. Humanism insists that personal liberty must be combined with social responsibility. Humanism ventures to build a world on the idea of the free person responsible to society, and recognises our dependence on and responsibility for the natural world. Humanism is undogmatic, imposing no creed upon its adherents. It is thus committed to education free from indoctrination.

  5. Humanism is a response to the widespread demand for an alternative to dogmatic religion. The world's major religions claim to be based on revelations fixed for all time, and many seek to impose their world-views on all of humanity. Humanism recognises that reliable knowledge of the world and ourselves arises through a continuing process of observation, evaluation and revision.

  6. Humanism values artistic creativity and imagination and recognises the transforming power of art. Humanism affirms the importance of literature, music, and the visual and performing arts for personal development and fulfilment.

  7. Humanism is a lifestance aiming at the maximum possible fulfilment through the cultivation of ethical and creative living and offers an ethical and rational means of addressing the challenges of our times. Humanism can be a way of life for everyone everywhere

Examples of famous humanists

Humanism has a long, established history and boasts a considerable list of famous and influential social and political figures. The famous ophthalmologist Fred Hollows, the first Director-General of the United Nation's Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO) Sir Julian Huxley , the scientist and author Isaac Asimov, Brock Chisholm, the first director General of the World Health Organisation, the philosopher Bertrand Russell, physician Jonas Stalk, human rights activist Phillip Randolf and author Margaret Atwood are a few examples of notable humanists.
