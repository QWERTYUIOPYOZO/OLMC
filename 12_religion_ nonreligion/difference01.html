Content Description

The concept of the transcendent

God created the world and gave meaning to life

All Christians believe in the concept of the transcendent, which is the idea that there is something outside of and beyond the human, and that it is this divine being which gives meaning and purpose to life. Christianity, which is a monotheistic religion, has termed this transcendent being, which exists, outside of and independent of the human, God. Christians believe that God is the source of creation and that God gives meaning and purpose to life.

Humanists reject the notion of the transcendent

Humanists on the other hand do not accept the idea that there is an ultimate reality, or that there is something beyond the ordinary experiences of human life. In fact, humanists have concluded, after an examination of evidence regarding the existence of a transcendent being that such evidence is inconclusive.

Life after physical death

Thus, according to humanists, this life and this world is all we have. The implications of this belief are that humanists reject any ideas about life after physical death. Christians on the other hand, because of their belief in the existence of a higher reality, a reality that exists outside of the human, believe in concepts such as heaven, hell, and judgement, as a part of their belief in eternal life after death.

Humanists, however, do not believe in the idea of life after physical death and thus would not even begin to entertain the notions of heaven, hell and judgement.

Source and mechanisms of creation

In a similar way, because humanists do not believe in the concept of the transcendent, humanists only accept the scientific view of evolution without any reference to God or to any other religious dimension.

Some fundamentalist groups within Christianity do not accept scientific views of the origin of the universe. Most Christians however, have no difficulty accepting evolution as the mechanism of creation while retaining a belief in God as the source of creation.

Thus, for humanists and many Christians, there is no argument about the mechanics of creation. The difference lies in what is believed to be behind this process. Secular humanists align themselves with some scientists who believe that creation was a spontaneous event without any motivation or cause. This is of course in stark contrast to Christians who believe that God is the author of creation. For those Christians who hold a fundamentalist / creationist view, there is a contrast with humanists in both the mechanics and the source of creation.

The human person

The human person is of paramount importance

Humanists and Christians alike regard the human person as being of supreme value. The humanist concept of the human person is essentially an optimistic one as it posits that human beings have thrived through their intelligent adaptation to the environment and their problem solving capabilities.

People have social responsibilities

Since both humanists and Christians acknowledge that the human person is of extreme value, it follows that both humanists and Christians hold the belief that people have certain communal or social responsibilities towards each other.

Needs of the individual are paramount

Following on from the humanist belief in the supreme value of the human person, another notable characteristic of the humanist understanding of the human person is the utilitarian belief in the idea that the needs of the individual should not be compromised other than for the greater good of society. This can be summed up with the principle, that the needs of the individual are paramount.

Source of this view

Christians and humanists differ somewhat in their view of the human person. For Christians, human beings are made in the likeness of God and hence are sacred. This additional dimension regarding the intrinsic sanctity of the human being is not present in humanist beliefs. Thus, humanists believe that communal and social responsibilities are derived purely from human experience, whereas for Christians such responsibilities are understood as being derived from the revelation of God.

Social responsibility

Belief that people have social responsibilities towards each other

Both Christians and humanists hold strong views on social responsibility and community involvement. A fundamental tenet of these worldviews is respect for all human beings, who are worthy of equal consideration, freedom of belief and expression, and tolerance of other beliefs and ways of life. This is because ultimately both belief systems aim to make the world a better place for all people to live in, and hence the improvement of the overall living of all people occupies a central position in both Christian and humanist thinking.

Source of this view

There are two significant points of disparity between the humanist and Christian view of social responsibility. The first difference relates to the nature of the doctrines the respective worldviews have regarding social responsibility. Most Christian groups have specific doctrines on social responsibility, which are set out in the Bible and in the teaching authorities of the tradition. Humanist doctrines, whilst they are similar, are not as clearly defined.

Life after physical death

The second point of divergence lies in the implications of the belief in the existence of a life after physical death. Humanists, because they have no belief in an afterlife, tend to place a greater emphasis on the contributions made by people in this life. Some Christian groups however, because they believe in the notion of life after death, have been criticised for placing too much emphasis on the concerns of the afterlife and therefore neglecting the needs of people in this life.

Material accumulation of goods

Social responsibility is a wide encompassing concept which involves addressing issues such as poverty, ecological concerns, world peace, justice and economic inequality. One important area of social action relates to the inequality in the distribution of material goods and possessions. Both groups have strong views on this issue, as both groups regard the excessive accumulation of possessions or resources as being harmful, particularly when there is an inequality in the distribution of these goods.

That is not to say however, that Christians and humanists reject the notion of material accumulation. In fact, both groups believe that material accumulation and possession is a natural part of life, it is only the excessive accumulation of such goods that needs to be addressed. This is particularly the case when the accumulation of wealth and possessions takes place at the expense of others who are deprived of the basic necessities of life.

Ecological sustainability

Another area of concern for the Christian and humanist community is ecological sustainability. There are distinct similarities in their practical approach towards ecological concerns as both regard the protection of the environment as critical. Hence, it is no surprise to find that humanists and Christians are both significant contributors to ecological activism. However, whilst there are distinct similarities between the Christians and humanist worldviews in their practical attitudes towards this issue there is a difference in the motivation behind their involvement.

Christians are assiduous advocates for environmental protection because they believe that the earth is a product of God and this relationship between the creator and creation needs to be sustained. Christians regard human beings as stewards of this earth and related to the rest of creation in a profound way. Humanists on the other hand, are motivated to protect the environment in the belief that human beings are dependant upon the environment for survival, but do not hold the same sense of relationship with a creator.

Both humanists and Christians have been criticised by some environmentalists both for past actions and present attitudes to the environment. Once again these criticisms highlight some similarities and differences. Humanists have been criticised in the past for holding a Cartesian view of the world, which encourages a human mastery over nature. Similarly Christian attitudes based on the idea of Genesis creation account calling for the "subduing the earth" can be seen in certain lights to encourage a kind of exploitation of the environment.

Other criticisms levelled at humanists focus on their emphasis on the human being as a kind of "speciesist" excess which tends to place other aspects of the environment at the service of humankind or which places such an emphasis on human values that it subordinates other aspects of the environment to the needs of human beings. In contrast, the Christian view of creation and the relationship with creator and the rest of creation have tended to enable it to avoid such criticism.
