Content Description

Importance of the Covenant
The Covenant, which lies at the heart of the Jewish religion, is a series of agreements recorded in the Hebrew Bible between God and the people of Israel. The importance of the Covenant cannot be overestimated because the Covenant, which is the cornerstone of Judaism, is the living expression of the relationship between God and the people of Israel.
Pattern of covenants
The expressions of the Covenant in the Tenak essentially follow the same pattern. They begin by describing or recalling God's great acts in the events of the everyday lives of the people. Next they contain commands for the people to follow. Finally, they conclude with a list of blessings and curses.
Noah - God's saving actions
There are four main expressions of the Covenant made between God and the people of Israel. The first expression is the Covenant made between God and Noah. God saves Noah and his family from the flood and in return Noah is obliged to live by caring for the earth, humankind, and all of creation.
Noah's covenantal obligations
In essence, Noah and his family are called to live in God's image and to walk in God's path. God warns Noah that if harmful acts are committed than God will exact vengeance for these acts. However, God also promises that if Noah is faithful to his covenantal obligations, than a disaster like the flood will not happen again. A rainbow appeared as a sign of this pact and the tradition teaches that the rainbow appearing in the sky is a sign of God remembering the Covenant.
Abraham - God's promise
The second expression of the Covenant recorded in the Hebrew Bible was between God and Abraham. Abraham is the first of the three Patriarchs of Judaism. Abraham (Abram) is called to leave his home and move to the land promised by God. The event is highly significant because it marks an agreement between Abraham and God which introduces two significant elements of the Covenant. These are the promises of land and of descendents.
Descendants
God promises that Abraham's descendants will be as numerous the "stars of the heavens and the grains of sand on the seashore" and Abraham, in return is called to be faithful to God and to walk in God's ways.
Canaan and Abraham's covenantal obligations
God also promises that Abraham will be the head of a great nation, and that his people will be given a land of their own, the land of Canaan, later known as Israel which is the modern day State of Israel and Palestinian Territories. In order to receive these blessings, like Noah, Abraham was obliged to walk in God's way, that is, to live in a righteous and just way. This spiritual covenant was sealed with a visible sign, that is, circumcision or brit milah. This physical sign of the covenant has been renewed by males in each generation.
Exodus event
The third expression of the Covenant relates to the agreement made between Moses, the People of Israel and God. This Covenant is the foundational event in the history of Judaism, as it underpins the entire religion.
God as the one who saves
The importance of the events associated with this expression of the Covenant is that they convey the notion of God's intervention in human history. This intervention provides a fundamental insight into the nature of God. Namely God is the one who saves. God's intervention in human history is a movement from slavery to freedom, from oppression to liberation. It is this understanding of the nature of God which has profoundly shaped the development of Judaism.
Obligation - acceptance of 10 commandments
The significance of the Exodus experience is found in the way in which the People of Israel understood and interpreted this event. God saved the people of Israel from slavery in Egypt, and the people of Israel in return were obliged to accept the Ten Commandments.
Significance of the covenant
This expression of the Covenant is highly significant for numerous reasons: Firstly, it shows that the people of Israel were to be the chosen people. Secondly it highlights God's care for the people of Israel in granting them freedom. In essence, the importance of the Covenant can be seen as thus: the Covenant is a reflection of the Israelites discovering a God who is interested in their welfare and gave them an identity by intervening in their fate.

The Covenant made with Moses on Mount Sinai was an extension and reaffirmation of the one made with Abraham. The people's agreement to it was expressed in the form of sacrifices to God. The blood from the sacrificed animals was splashed on the people to consecrate them with God. That is, they were distinguished from other people in the area and regarded themselves as the People of God.

Covenant with King David
In the later parts of the Tenak there are similar but more particular expressions of the Covenant in terms of King David. David is a humble shepherd boy and God chooses him to be a powerful and victorious king. In return, David has to walk in God's way by being a just and fair ruler.
Essence of covenantal obligations
The Covenant is significant because it expresses the ongoing relationship between God and the people of Israel that lies at the very core of the Jewish tradition. The common obligation that is similar to all these expressions of the Covenant is to walk in God's way and to act in such a way that reflections their creation in God's image and likeness.
Exile and restoration
Intimately tied to the theme of the Covenant is the notion of exile and restoration. The contours of Jewish history reflect this theme, as the people are blessed when they are faithful and suffer the consequences when they are unfaithful.
God is always faithful
Finally, it should be noted that the Covenant reflects an important attribute of God, that God always remains faithful regardless of whether the people are faithful to the covenant. In other words, God is always ready to embrace the people even when they turn their backs on God.
Observance
In the lived expression of Judaism, the idea of Covenant remains central. The observance of the mitzvot of the Torah is essentially the response to the requirements of the Sinai Covenant. Observant Jews will seek to keep the Torah in order to fulfill the requirements of the Covenant.
