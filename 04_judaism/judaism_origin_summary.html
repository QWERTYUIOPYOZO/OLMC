Summary Points

Students learn about:

Abraham and the Covenant.

Students learn to:

Outline the life of Abraham.

  • The origins of Judaism can be attributed to the Patriarchs (Abraham, Jacob and Isaac).
  • Abraham's encounters with God:
    1. God promises Abraham that:
      1. He will become the head of a great nation.
      2. God will provide him with a land of his own, the Promised Land of Canaan. God then instructs him to leave his own territory in southern Mesopotamia.
    2. In Hebron, God promises that:
      1. Abraham's descendents will be as numerous as the stars, in response to Abraham's plea for a child from his marriage to Sarah, despite their advanced years.
    3. Following the birth of Ishmael, Abraham encounters God again where the promise of descendants is reaffirmed. Abraham is obliged to walk in the presence of God and be blameless. This covenant is known as the covenant of circumcision (brit milah).
    4. God requests the sacrifice of Isaac, the only son of Abraham and Sarah, and Abraham complies with this command. At the last minute the angel of God intervenes and saves Isaac from death. God reaffirms the dual promise of descendants and land in response to Abraham's fidelity.

Students learn about:

Abraham and the Covenant.

Students learn to:

Describe the Covenant with the Patriarchs, including the promises of a People and a Land.

  • Importance of the covenant - the Covenant lies at the heart of the Jewish religion.
  • It expresses the intimate relationship between God and the people of Israel that is the cornerstone of Judaism.
  • The keeping of the mitzvot of the Torah is now the fundamental measure of fidelity to the Covenant.
  • There are four major covenants recorded in the Hebrew Bible:
    1. Noah
      • God's saving actions - Saves Noah and his family from the flood.
      • God's promise - The earth will be destroyed again by flood.
      • Obligation - Noah and his descendants are called upon to live according to the ways of God, by tending to and caring for God's creation.
    2. Abraham
      • God's promise -
        1. Abraham's descendants will be as numerous as the stars and will lead to the formation of a great nation. This will begin with the birth of Isaac, whose son Jacob is the father of the twelve tribes of Israel.
        2. To live in the promised land of Canaan.
      • Obligation - Abraham is required in the presence of God and be blameless.
    3. Sinai covenant with Moses following the Exodus.
    4. Covenant with David regarding the Monarchy.

Students learn about:

Moses, the Exodus and the giving of the Torah.

Students learn to:

Outline the story of the Exodus and the giving of the Law at Sinai, including the Ten Commandments.

  • Moses' childhood - He was a Hebrew who grew up in the Egyptian royal court after being adopted by the Pharaoh's daughter.
  • He was forced to flee Egypt after having killed an Egyptian.
  • Moses' first encounter with God - During his sojourn in Midian, Moses encountered God who gave Moses the task of liberating the Hebrew people from slavery in Egypt.
  • Following the tenth plague by God which led to the death of the first born in every Egyptian family the Pharaoh let the Hebrews who were held in captivity, free.
  • The angel of God passed over the houses belonging to the Hebrews, which were marked with blood, sparing their first born.
  • Pharaoh sent his army in pursuit of the Hebrews, who were now free to flee slavery in Egypt.
  • The Egyptian army drowned when the Red Sea, which was parted by God to allow the Hebrews to cross to the other side, closed in on them.
  • Following the crossing of the Red Sea, God revealed the Ten Commandments to Moses at Mount Sinai.
  • The liberation from slavery, known as the Exodus is a defining and foundational moment in the Jewish tradition and is seen as foundational for the tradition.
  • Significance of the Exodus - The Hebrews discovered a God who was intimately concerned with their welfare and intervened in their fate by freeing them from oppression.
  • The theme of freedom from oppression is a central concept that underpins Jewish ethics.
  • The Ten Commandments which form the basis of the Sinai law (613 mitzvot) is significant because signifies the ratification of the Covenant, and governs the day to day life of a Jew.

Students learn about:

Modern Judaism

  • Conservative Judaism
  • Orthodox Judaism
  • Progressive Judaism

Students learn to:

Outline the unique features of Conservative Judaism, Orthodox Judaism, and Progressive Judaism

  • The decisive factor that differentiates between the different Jewish groupings is how strictly they apply the halakhah, which is the collective body of Jewish rabbinic law and tradition.
  • Conservative Judaism
    • Founded by Zacharias Frankel in Germany during the 19 th century.
    • Conservative Jews are still bound to follow the halakhic laws, even though the Torah is written by divinely inspired human beings.
    • Halakhah is a product of the socio-cultural conditions of the time. Hence, it is capable of being reinterpreted in light of changing circumstances.
  • Orthodox Judaism
    • Strict observance of the commandments as they represent the direct will of God.
    • 2 major divisions within contemporary Orthodox Judaism.
    • Modern Orthodox Judaism - Aims to maintain Orthodox requirements whilst seeking to live as part of the modern world.
    • Ultra Orthodox Judaism - Imposes a strict ban on Jews from participating with secular society. Eg. Hasidic Jews, founded by the Besht in the 18 th century.
    • Recognisable by their distinctive dress which includes long black coats, round black hats, beards and side locks.
  • Progressive Judaism
    • Includes Reconstructionist and Reform strands.
    • Reject concept of divine revelation as a direct dictation of the Tenak by God ie it does not represent the literal word of God.
    • Torah is instructional but not binding in the literal sense.
    • Movement away from strict halakhic observance.
    • The rejection of halakhic laws particularly pronounced in the early years of its development.
