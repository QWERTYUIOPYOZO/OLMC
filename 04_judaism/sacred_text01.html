Content Description

Tenak
The Hebrew bible, known as the Tenak is made up of three sections, each containing a number of books which are in turn divided into chapters and verses.

The first section is the Torah which is essentially the books of the Jewish Law. The second section is the Nevi'im which contains the books of the Prophets. The third section is the Ketuvim which contains a collection of other writings loosely described as Wisdom literature. The most well known of the books of the Ketuvim are those of the Psalms and Proverbs.

The name of the Hebrew bible comes from the first letter of the name of each section. T for Torah, N for Nevi'im and K for Ketuvim. This forms the acronym TNK. Vowels are added to provide for pronunciation and the word Tenak (TeNaK) is formed. The Tenak is often simply referred to as the Bible or sometimes the Hebrew bible.

It is also a custom at times to use the term "Torah" to refer generally to the whole Hebrew bible. This is because the Torah is the central element of the Hebrew bible and the section which is most highly esteemed and warrants the most study.

Torah - The most important part of the Tenak
The first section of the Tenak is the Torah. It contains 5 books which are variously known as the books of the law, the books of Moses and the Pentateuch. The Torah is the most important part of the Tenak because it contains the expression of the Covenant between God and the people of Israel and it sets out the law which Jewish people are obliged to follow in order to keep the Covenant.
Covenantal obligations
The expression of the Covenant is found principally in the book of Genesis, the first book of the Torah. There are, however, various occasions in the other books of the Torah where the people are called upon to renew their commitment to the Covenant. Thus there are a number of eloquent expressions of the Covenant found throughout the books of the Torah such as in Deuteronomy chapters 5 and 6 and especially in the words of the "shema" (Deuteronomy 6:4-9).
613 mitzvot
The books of the Torah also set out the requirements of keeping the Covenant in terms of the 613 mitzvot or commandments. These mitzvot, beginning with the 10 Commandments (Exodus 20:1-17) set out all the requirements expected of the people of Israel in order to be faithful to the Covenant. These mitzvot regulate all aspects of Jewish life including religious rituals, interpersonal relationships, ethics etc.
Observance related to 613 mitzvot
For an observant Jewish person keeping the mitzvot are of fundamental importance. They will study the Torah to understand what is required and faithfully attempt to follow these requirements. All aspects of observance are related to the 613 mitzvot of the Torah. Accordingly it is impossible to overestimate the significance of the Torah for Jewish life.
Nevi'im
The second element of the Tenak is the Nevi'im. This section contains the writings of the Prophets and is divided into two groups known as the Major Prophets and the Minor Prophets.
Prophetic tradition
In history, the role of the Prophets was essentially to call Israel to fidelity with the Covenant. Throughout the history of Israel at various times the people fell away from their observance of the Torah and the Prophets were responsible for pointing out this transgression and calling for repentance and a return to Covenant fidelity. Often this call was accompanied by warnings of dire consequences if Israel failed to repent.

The writings of the Prophets are therefore a constant reminder to Jewish people of the importance of remaining faithful to the Covenant and also of the blessings and curses associated with Covenant fidelity or the lack thereof.

Nevi'im is secondary to the Torah
The Nevi'im, therefore, plays a supporting role to the Torah. It calls Jewish people to account and reminds them of their obligation to the Covenant.
Ketuvim
The third section of the Tenak is the Ketuvim or "writings". The Ketuvim is a collection of books which are loosely referred to as Wisdom literature. The most significant parts of the Ketuvim are the books of Psalms and Proverbs. The book of Psalms contains a mix of hymns of praise and lament. They are extremely important for their role in worship and prayer. The Proverbs are collections of short pithy statements expressing practical wisdom. They are not the type of text requiring theological exegesis, rather they are simple, down to earth pronouncements relating to prudent living.
Tenak is of critical importance to Judaism
Overall the Hebrew bible has a central role in the Jewish religion. Principally this is through the foundational role of the Torah in Jewish life. The other parts of the Tenak are also important but can be seen primarily as supporting the role of the Torah.
Oral Torah
Another important element of Jewish sacred texts and writings is the area known as Oral Torah. The Oral Torah is essentially the interpretation and application of the Torah to changing circumstances over successive generations. It was inevitable that a written text would reflect the circumstances in which it was written, yet the enduring principles and precepts of the Torah needed to be applied to each new generation. This need brought about the Oral Torah.

Almost immediately there was need of interpretation of the Torah so that it could be meaningfully applied in each circumstance. This interpretation was the domain of the Jewish authorities who spent countless hours of study and debate considering the most appropriate application of the Torah.

Mishnah
The decisions of these authorities were originally passed on in oral form and later came to be written down in collections that could be reliably passed on from generation to generation. The most important of these collections was known as the Mishnah. The Mishnah contained expositions and interpretations of legal sections of the books of Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers and Deuteronomy, The Mishnah was finalised in written form around 200 CE.
Gemara
The next significant collection of the Oral Torah is known as the Gemara. The Gemara is essentially a commentary on the Mishnah with further interpretation and elaboration of important texts. The Gemara was developed between 300 and 500 CE.
Talmud
The two parts of Oral Torah, Mishnah and Gemara, were finally collected together in the Talmud during the 5th and 6th centuries CE. The word "Talmud" is an Aramaic term which means learning. The Talmud is an enormous volume of important literature for Jewish life. It contains compilations of the law with detailed elucidations from the wisdom of the Rabbis. It also contains history, stories and theological reflection.
Halakhah
The Talmud essentially contains two types of subject matter. The first is known as Halakhah. This is the legal component of the Talmud. The Halakhah is comprised of the rules and laws for living. The term "Halakhah" means going or walking". For Jewish people, the Halakhah provides the detailed interpretation and application of the 613 Mitzvot of the Torah which enables them to know the appropriate response to situations that arise. In modern times, Jewish legal authorities known as Halakhic lawyers are able to provide expert guidance in understanding the requirements of Halakhah.
Haggadah
The second type of subject matter in the Talmud is known as Haggadah which means "telling". The Haggadah includes all the stories, narrations, legend etc. While the Haggadah is important in providing wisdom and insight into principles of Jewish life, it does not have the prescriptive force of the legal requirements of Halakhah.
Bavli and Yerushalmi
For historical reasons the Talmud actually exists in two forms or two versions. A Babylonian version known as "Bavli" was developed between 400 and 700 CE while the Palestinian version known as "Yerushalmi" was developed between 400 and 600 CE.

Both versions of the Talmud are acknowledged in Jewish communities, however, usually one has a more prominent place in keeping with the historical and cultural heritage of the particular community.

Systematic codification of the Talmud
While the Talmud is an extremely important source of Jewish sacred texts and writings, it is also a particularly difficult source to use. This is due to the absence of any systematic codification of the material contained in the Talmud. The absence of this codification meant that the Rabbis were required to undertake painstaking study to be familiar with all the teachings of the Talmud on any given matter which may be scattered throughout the vast text.

The work of Moses Maimonides in the 11th century CE and his development of the Mishneh Torah has greatly assisted in the study and use of the Talmud.

Talmud is secondary to the Torah
While the Talmud is the focus of much study in Jewish communities, it is important to recognise that the Talmud is always at the service of the Written Torah. The Torah is the primary document of Jewish sacred texts and writings. It is the Torah that expresses the Covenant and provides the Mitzvot for living. The Talmud is its supporter and companion work. The Talmud draws from the Torah and relies on the Torah. It seeks only to make clear the way for Jewish people to live the requirements of the Covenant as conveyed in the Torah.
