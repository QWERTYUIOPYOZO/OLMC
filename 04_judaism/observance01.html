Content Description

God rested on the 7th day of creation
The practice of Shabbat is derived from the Biblical story where God rested on the seventh day after creating the world and hence people are called to imitate God by resting every seventh day. Thus, the Sabbath is a day of rest and rejuvenation, a day of family and community and a time to study the Torah and attend the synagogue for Jewish people.

This view of the Sabbath as a holy and special day is expressed in Exodus 20:8 which calls people to "Remember the Sabbath and keep it holy". The Sabbath is a most significant liturgical event of the week because it reminds the Jewish people of the greatness of God in creating the world and humankind, and the importance of the Covenant between God and the People of Israel, forged following the exodus from slavery.

Covenantal obligations
In Exodus 31:16 the people are reminded to "Observe the Sabbath day throughout the generations as an everlasting covenant". The importance of Shabbat is clearly evident in that it is affectionately referred to by the Jewish people as the Queen of Days.
2 primary Shabbat commandments
The requirements for keeping the Sabbath can be summed up by two commandments, firstly "Remember the Sabbath day" (Exodus 20:8) which means refraining from all forms of work, and secondly "Keep the Sabbath day" (Deuteronomy 5:12) which refers to reciting the Kiddush over a cup of wine.

This emphasis on ritual highlights the fact that Judaism is largely concerned with carrying out halakhic requirements as a reflection of faith rather than being a religion that is focused on a detailed theological exploration of the concepts which underpin the religion. Admittedly, whilst this is important, conceptual analysis is of secondary importance compared to how these concepts can be brought into practice for believers on a day to day basis.

All forms of work are prohibited
In relation to the negative mitzvot of ceasing from all forms of work, it should be noted that all kinds of work and labor are prohibited on the Sabbath. In essence, by ceasing all forms of work Jews are acknowledging God as the ultimate creator and that whilst human beings are partners with God in this creation, the act of creation is a gift from God and not an automatic right.

There are four general categories of work listed in the Torah which are prohibited on the Sabbath. Talmudic scholars have expanded on these four basic categories by drawing up a list of thirty nine prohibited categories. Directly tied to these prohibitions are utensils and objects which cannot be handled on the Sabbath. Such objects are known as "muktzeh" which can literally be translated as "that which is set aside".

Considering the importance of ritualistic observance in Judaism it is not surprising to find that Rabbinic scholars have written extensively about the types of activities which are forbidden on the Sabbath, particularly in the light of the exponential technological changes that have taken place in society, as the number of forbidden activities will continue to grow under the different existing categories of prohibited activities.

Sabbath delight and honour
Even though ritual observance is of primary importance for Jews, this observance needs to be accompanied by genuine delight in honouring and keeping the Sabbath. For example, the Prophet Isaiah teaches "If thou turn away thy foot because of the Sabbath, from pursuing thy business on my holy day; and call the Sabbath a delight, And the holy of the Lord honorable, And shall honor it, not doing thy wonted ways, not pursuing the business, nor speaking thereof" (Isaiah 58:3).

This passage draws attention to two important underlying principles of Sabbath observance, firstly the notion of Sabbath delight or oneg Shabbat and Sabbath honor, or kavod Shabbat.

Sabbath candles
There are various rituals associated with the Sabbath. Just before the Sabbath is about to start the wife lights two candles. The first candle is meant to represent the injunction to keep the Sabbath and the second candle represents the commandment to remember the Sabbath. The wife then prays for the welfare of her husband and children over the lit candles. A short synagogue service takes place at sunset to mark the beginning of the Sabbath.
Kiddush
The most important celebration that takes place on the Friday of the Sabbath is the Shabbat dinner. This takes place at home with family and friends. The Shabbat table is laid with two loaves, known as "hallot" which, like the candles, represent the dual Shabbat commandments to keep and remember Sabbath, and cups, over which Kiddush is recited. The father blesses the children in order of age, before Kiddush is recited over the wine, which "gladdens the heart" (Psalm 104:15).
Synagogue attendance
On the following day, two meals are eaten, one following the morning synagogue service and the second meal is eaten in the afternoon, generally with coffee and cake. The former meal is preceded by Kiddush. Saturday morning is usually spent in Torah study at the synagogue. The highlight of the service is the reading of a specified segment of the Torah.
Havdalah
The appearance of three stars in the darkened sky at sunset marks the end of the Shabbat. At this point, havdalah is recited. Essentially, havdalah which means "division" is a recitation which praises God for creating distinctions, between what is considered sacred and what is not and between light and darkness for example. The braided havdalah candle is now lit, as a sign that Shabbat has ended, hence lighting a fire is permissible again.
