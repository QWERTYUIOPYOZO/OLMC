Content Description

Strictness of halakhic observance as the definitive factor
Before considering the distinguishing characteristics of the major groupings within Judaism it will be helpful to briefly survey the contours of history which gave rise to the existence of different groups in the Jewish tradition. The essential factor that differentiates between the Jewish groupings is how strictly they apply the halakhah, that is, the collective body of Jewish rabbinic law and tradition.

Progressive Judaism

1) Reform Judaism

Oral and written law do not represent literal word of God
Reform Judaism developed in nineteenth century German as a result of the experience of the Haskalah, which is also known as the Jewish Enlightenment. Later in the century Reform Judaism was brought to America by German immigrants. Reform Judaism is characterised by its rejection of the concept of divine revelation as a direct dictation of the Tenak by God. This is the notion that the Written Law is the literal word of God and similarly, that the Oral Law is an extension of this divine revelation. Rather Reform Judaism holds the belief that the Torah was written by divinely inspired human beings. The implication of this core belief is that the Torah is inspirational and instructional, but not binding in a literal sense, since it is not the literal word of God.
Rejection of strict halakhic observance
This amounted to a movement away from strict of halakhic observance. The halakhah encompasses a broad spectrum of laws regulating dietary requirements, sexual and familial purity. It also formalised the existence of a priestly caste and codes of dress. The reform movement's rejection of halakhic laws was most pronounced in the early years of its development, as seen in the 1885 Pittsburgh Platform, which is the founding document of American Reform Judaism. The rejection of strict halakhic observance, whilst explicit was not absolute, as the ethical commandments set out in the Torah were still considered binding.
Other significant beliefs
Other notable beliefs of Reform Judaism, which differentiate it from traditional Judaism include its rejection of the notion of a personal Messiah, the resurrection of the dead, the return to the Holy Land, the rebuilding of the Temple in Jerusalem, and the reintroduction of Temple sacrifice.
Innovations in ritual practice
Reform Judaism throughout its history has also made significant reforms in terms of ritual practice. Innovations made by Reform Judaism include introducing religious service in the vernacular of the community instead of Hebrew and men and women sitting together during worship. In more recent times, Reform Judaism has also pioneered and pushed for the ordination of women, the use of the organ in services, the Bat Mitzvah and the confirmation ceremony. Reform Judaism in modern times is also recognised for its active involvement in social justice issues.
Reform Judaism is increasingly diverse in modern times
Reform Judaism has become increasingly diverse in recent times, and will continue to expand and grow as a pluralistic movement. In fact, there are few definitive norms which all members adhere to. One segment of Reform Judaism is retreating back towards tradition and is beginning to reintegrate some of the halakhic practices originally rejected by the founders of Reform Judaism. Furthermore, since the Holocaust, Reform Judaism despite the rejection of the belief in the return to the Holy Land has been more sympathetic to the Zionist cause.

2) Reconstructionism

Oral and written law do not represent the literal word of God
The Reconstructionist movement was founded by Mordecai Kaplan in the 1920s with the organisational structure of the group being formalised in the 1950s and 1960s. Reconstructionism has a limited number of members because it is largely a movement of intellectuals. Like the Reform and Conservative Judaism, Reconstructionists reject the notion that the Written and Oral Torah was the product of direct dictation from God, but believe that such texts were written by divinely inspired human beings. Hence they do not represent the literal word of God.
Judaism as a religion and civilisation
Kaplan held the belief that the Torah is "the expression of ancient Israel's attempt to base his life on a declaration of dependence on God, and on a constitution which embodies the laws according to which God expected ancient Israel to live". To sum this up, in essence Reconstructionism is centered on the belief that "the past has a vote, not a veto" in asserting that Judaism is not merely a religion, it is a culture and civilisation.
Zionism
It is not surprising, therefore, that Zionism features most prominently in Reconstructionist thought. Zionism in essence is about striving towards achieving the establishment of a Jewish state. Zionism is not a strand within Judaism, rather it is a political philosophy which features in the thinking of many Jewish groups.

Zionist philosophy also influences the way Reconstructionists understand the mitzvot, which literally means commandments. Reconstructionists argue that considering the evolution of Judaism, not all of the original 613 commandments will be applicable to our present day society.

Accordingly, Reconstructionists have devised criteria to determine which mitzvoth are applicable to society today. The first criterion is whether the commandment helps to build up the cultural identity of the Jewish people as a civilisation. The second criterion builds upon the first, as it questions whether the commandment assists in the attainment of personal and group wisdom.

God as a process
Reconstructionism is so significantly similar to Conservative Judaism that many have described Reconstructionism as Conservative Judaism minus the belief in a personal God. Reconstructionists believe that God is not a supernatural being rather God is seen as a process or power that is the sum of all forces that gives life meaning and worth.

Conservative Judaism

Associations with Reform Judaism
Conservative Judaism began in Germany in the mid to late nineteenth century as a reaction towards Reform Judaism and its explicit and outright rejection of halakhic practice. Its founder, Zacharias Frankel, initially joined the Reform Movement in rejecting the belief that the Written Law was the literal word of God in favor of the notion that authorship should be attributed to divinely inspired human beings.
Reinterpretation of halakhic law
Not long after, a group of less radical Reform Jews broke away from Reform Judaism in the belief that they were still bound to follow the ritual law, that is, that they were still bound to follow the halakhah. As a consequence, Conservative Judaism still holds the Oral Law as the authoritative source for halakhah. Conservative Judaism which today constitutes the largest grouping of Jews in the US was brought to America in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
Comparison with Orthodox Judaism
The core belief of Conservative Judaism is the belief that the ritual law whilst binding is open to interpretation and it is up to each new generation to make the halakhic law applicable to the age. That is not to suggest that the application of the halakhic laws is purely arbitrary. In fact, Torah study involves a rigorous study of the socio-cultural conditions in which it was written and consequently, how it can be applied to the modern times.

In this way Conservative Judaism differs from Orthodox Judaism in that whilst both acknowledge the importance of the halakhah in regulating the day to day life of a Jew, Conservative Jews believe that the interpretation of these laws are ever-evolving and capable of being adjusted to contemporary needs. This is based on the understanding that the halakhah itself is a product of the socio-cultural conditions of the time and not set in concrete.

Thus, Conservative Judaism has joined with the Reform and Reconstructionist groups within Judaism in some of its reforms such as ordaining women as rabbis and cantors.

Other notable features
Other distinguishing features of Conservative Judaism include the fact that it holds services in the vernacular and includes prayers in the vernacular at other times when most of the service is in Hebrew. Like Reform groups, Conservative synagogues use the organ in services. Conservative Judaism also emphasises the importance of the land of Israel and the continuation of the Jewish tradition.

Orthodox Judaism

Strict observance of mitzvot
Orthodox Judaism is the name given to what has been mainstream Rabbinic Judaism for centuries. Essentially, Orthodox Judaism is characterised by a strict observance of the commandments as they see these commandments or mitzvot as the direct will of God.
Divisions within Orthodox Judaism
There are two distinct groups within Orthodox Judaism, Ultra-Orthodox Judaism and Modern Orthodox Judaism. Modern Orthodoxy is a strand of Judaism which while maintaining Orthodox requirements seeks to live as part of the modern world. Ultra Orthodox on the other hand, believes that the only way to ensure the survival of the Jewish people and its religion is to impose a strict separation on Jews from participating with secular society.
Ultra Orthodox Judaism
The position of Ultra Orthodox Judaism has hardened in response to the growing influence held by Reform and Conservative Judaism. The Hasidic Jews (sometimes spelt Chasidic) are usually regarded as an example of Ultra Orthodox Judaism. They are recognisable by their distinctive dress which includes long black coats, round black hats, beards and side locks.

Hasidism was founded by Bal Shem Tov in the eighteenth century among Jews who lived in ghettos and who were poor and illiterate as a result of centuries of persecution. The Besht, as Bal Shem Tov became known, sought to rejuvenate many of the essential elements of Jewish law by emphasising in his teachings joy, love and awe of God, attachment/devotion (devekut) to God. Since the destruction of the Eastern Jewry in Europe, the US and Israel have become the centres of Hasidic worship.

Modern Orthodox Judaism
While the ultra Orthodox groups within Judaism seek to keep themselves separate from the influences of the prevailing culture, Modern Orthodox Judaism is immersed in the surrounding culture while at the same time maintaining faithful observance of the Torah.

Accordingly, among Modern Orthodox communities Jews will be found involved in a range of professions and involved in most aspects of modern community life. Alongside this immersion in modern society the Modern Orthodox Jew seeks to maintain observance of the Torah through carefully attending to the dietary, ritual and ethical requirements of the tradition.
